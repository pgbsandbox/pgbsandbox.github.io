Tasks

Tasks should form the core of your documentation.

Why and who

A task is any explanation of how to do something with the product. In most documentation, tasks form the core of the documentation because they tell readers how to do something specific with the product. All other topics support the tasks by explaining the underlying ideas or providing additional information that is necessary to understanding what is in the tasks themselves.

One of the primary reasons readers access the documentation is because they need help doing something with your product. If you write clear, step-by-step tasks that tell them exactly how to do the thing they want to do, your documentation becomes immensely useful and valuable.

Examples of tasks (procedures)

Some examples of tasks include:
  • Setting up a profile
  • Logging in
  • Customizing the product
  • Generating reports
  • Assembling something

Keeping it procedural

There are two rules of thumb to follow to keeping tasks focused on how to information:
  • If you are telling the reader how to do something, that information must be in a task (and nowhere else).
  • A task should include the steps the reader should follow and just enough extra information to be able to perform those steps; anything additional should go in the supporting concepts or reference topics.

Processes

A secondary use of task topics is to explain a process, which is a description of how something occurs. A process has steps too, but these are not steps that the reader necessarily performs (although they may perform some of them). The actor, or the person who performs the step, tends to change in a process.

Examples of processes

Some examples of processes include:
  • How ticket submission works
  • How an eyeball sees light
  • How a company hires new employees
  • How service delivery occurs
