Write great tasks

The best documentation focuses on goal-oriented tasks.

The primary reason a reader accesses documentation is to get help figuring out how to use the product. The reader has a problem with the product, they spend a minute or two trying to figure it out on their own, and then they try the documentation. If the documentation provides answers, then it is useful and usable. The key is to identify how your readers use your product so you can anticipate the help they need.

  1. Gather as much information about the product users as you can.
  2. Considering product functionality, make a list of related business goals the reader might perform.
    For example, if the product functionality is creating reports, then business goals could include:
    • Create line-of-business reports
    • Aggregate information in one report
    • Export reports to Excel
    • Create end-of-week reports
    • Create automatic, nightly reports

    Your business goal can and often does involve two or more pieces of product functionality working together.

  3. Verify with the development team and other key stakeholders that the business goals are valid and likely.
    This opens up a discussion that not only lets you refine your ideas but helps you identify even more business goals.
  4. Prioritize the business goals by how likely they are and how difficult they are to perform.
    This gives you a list of business goals, organized by importance and usefulness. The topics at the top of this list are the ones you should definitely write.
  5. Following your prioritized list, write a task that walks the reader through exactly how to do accomplish that business goal.
    Make sure a topic remains focused on that one business goal. Don't try to write for multiple goals at once.
  6. Create supporting concept and reference topics for information that is not procedural but is related to the task.
    For example, if an interface page has a series of options, put those options and their brief descriptions in a reference topic. If they need longer descriptions and explanations, write a concept that includes those as well.
